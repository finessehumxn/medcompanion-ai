# Content & Publisher Partnerships — strategy (how to get what OpenEvidence has)

**Honest framing:** Deals with NEJM, JAMA, NCCN, Wiley, Cochrane, Elsevier, etc. are **enterprise content-licensing contracts** — negotiated business agreements, not something you build. They require a credible company, legal/BD effort, money (licensing fees and/or revenue share), and usually **funding + traction + warm intros**. This is a post-raise growth lever, sequenced correctly.

## Why OpenEvidence has them (set expectations honestly)
OpenEvidence landed those agreements from a position of strength: a **Mayo Clinic Platform** company, **very large funding** (hundreds of millions), a **big clinician user base**, and a clinician-facing product publishers trust to represent their content well. Publishers license to partners who have **scale, money, credibility, and proper IP handling.** We get there in stages — we do not start there.

## Stage 1 — what we use NOW (free / open, fully legitimate)
The app already does live web search and cites sources. Lean on **authoritative, freely-usable** sources and cite/link them (do not reproduce paywalled text):
- **NIH / MedlinePlus**, **PubMed / PMC open-access subset**, **CDC**, **FDA drug labels (DailyMed)**, **WHO**
- **Publicly posted clinical guidelines** from specialty societies
- **Cochrane plain-language summaries** (many are openly available)
- Note: Mayo Clinic, UpToDate, NEJM, JAMA full text are **copyrighted** — link and cite, never reproduce verbatim. Keep our "we cite sources you can check" model.
This is credible enough to launch and to tell investors the truth: *premium licensing is a growth lever, not a launch blocker.*

## Stage 2 — pursue paid licensing (post-funding / with traction)
Order targets from most-accessible to hardest:
| Target | Why / how to approach |
|---|---|
| **Wiley** | Has publicly done AI content-licensing deals; has a rights & licensing arm. Most AI-friendly first call. |
| **Cochrane** | Licensing available; mission-aligned with plain-language patient understanding. |
| **NCCN** | Licenses its oncology guidelines through a formal licensing program. |
| **Elsevier / ScienceDirect / ClinicalKey** | Large catalog; enterprise licensing team. |
| **BMJ, Springer Nature, Wolters Kluwer (UpToDate/DynaMed)** | Established licensing programs. |
| **NEJM Group (Mass. Medical Society), JAMA (AMA)** | Premium, hardest; pursue after scale + credibility. |
Each has a **"content licensing," "rights & permissions," or "partnerships"** team — that is who you contact, not editorial.

## What you need before you ask
1. **Funding earmarked** for licensing fees / minimum guarantees (these deals cost money).
2. **IP/contracts counsel** experienced in content licensing.
3. A **content-use plan**: how you display, attribute, and avoid improper reproduction; data security.
4. **Traction + credibility**: a real product, user numbers, and your **Medical Advisory Board** as proof you will represent content responsibly.
5. **Warm intros** beat cold outreach: use **investors, advisors, your physician board, and accelerators** (e.g., Mayo Clinic Platform_Accelerate — the program OpenEvidence came through).

## Phased plan
- **Now:** ship on free/open authoritative sources + citations. Build users and credibility.
- **Raise:** include a content-licensing line item and name target publishers in the deck.
- **Post-raise:** hire/contract BD + IP counsel; start with Wiley/Cochrane/NCCN; expand to premium (NEJM/JAMA) as scale grows.

## Investor soundbite
> "Today we ground answers in authoritative public sources (NIH, FDA, CDC, PubMed) with citations. Premium publisher licensing (Wiley, Cochrane, NCCN, then NEJM/JAMA) is a funded growth lever we pursue post-raise, via warm intros and our medical board — the same path category leaders took once they had scale."
