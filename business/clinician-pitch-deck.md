## Slide 2 — The Problem

# Patients don't actually understand what just happened to them

- **The comprehension gap is real.** Patients nod, say "okay," and leave — then can't explain their own diagnosis to a family member an hour later.
- **The "white coat" effect.** Anxiety, jargon, and time pressure mean a large share of what's said in the room is never retained. The patient is too overwhelmed to ask.
- **Low health literacy is the norm, not the exception.** Even native English speakers struggle with terms like "hypertension," "benign," "lipid panel," or "ejection fraction."
- **Language barriers compound everything.** Limited-English-proficiency (LEP) patients get an even thinner slice of the message — and ad-hoc interpretation is inconsistent.
- **Written instructions don't fix it.** Standard after-visit summaries are generated for the chart and the coder, not written *to* the patient at their reading level, in their language.

*Speaker note:* Keep this slide empathetic, not accusatory. The clinician did everything right; the *communication medium* failed. That reframing is what makes a busy medical director lean in instead of getting defensive.

## Slide 5 — How It Works

# The clinician workflow: three steps, seconds long

1. **Find the answer (as you do today).** Clinician reaches a diagnosis or reviews a result — using their judgment, plus tools like OpenEvidence/UpToDate if they wish.
2. **Explain it in plain language.** Drop the finding into MedCompanion. Instantly get a clear, jargon-free explanation tuned to patient comprehension — and pick the patient's language.
3. **Hand it over.** Generate a co-branded handout in the patient's language. Print it, text it, or surface it in the app. The patient leaves with something they understand.

**Designed to fit the visit, not interrupt it:**
- Fast enough to use *during* the encounter, not as after-hours homework.
- Clinician reviews and can edit before anything reaches the patient — the human stays in the loop.
- Multilingual output means no scrambling for an interpreter just to explain a result.

*Speaker note:* Emphasize "human in the loop." Clinicians and risk officers both need to hear that the doctor reviews/edits output before it's handed to the patient — MedCompanion drafts the explanation; the clinician approves it.
